# Free Will Boundary  
*A Congruity 7.0 regime analysis*

## Overview
Free will is traditionally framed as a dilemma:
- either fully determined by prior physical states
- or fundamentally random and uncaused

Both framings fail because they treat agency as an internal mechanism.

This repository reframes free will as a **boundary condition**, not a force or exception.

---

## Congruity Framing

Using the canonical Congruity relation:

\[
C = \frac{V}{E + I + S}
\]

agency arises in regimes where **functional choice cannot be compressed into predictive structure without loss of coherence**.

Free will marks the point where decision-making remains admissible only from the inside.

---

## Determinism Is Insufficient

Strict determinism assumes:
- full informational closure
- complete predictability from prior states

But perfect prediction requires:
- infinite informational access
- infinite computational resolution

Both violate congruity constraints.

Thus determinism collapses under bounded intelligence.

---

## Randomness Is Insufficient

Pure randomness removes responsibility:
- no continuity of agency
- no coherence over time

Randomness substitutes control with noise, breaking value formation.

Free will is neither determinism nor randomness.

---

## Agency as Boundary Phenomenon

Agency exists when:
- multiple future trajectories are physically admissible
- selection occurs without external specification
- choice remains internally coherent but externally underdetermined

This is not metaphysical freedom —  
it is **structural non-reducibility under bounded conditions**.

---

## Relation to Consciousness

Without first-person perspective:
- no valuation
- no preference
- no responsibility

Thus free will is inseparable from consciousness,
but not reducible to neural causation.

---

## Implications

- Free will does not violate physical law  
- It violates the assumption of total observability  
- Prediction fails not because of chaos, but because of boundary limits  

Any complete model must account for this admissibility gap.
